How RAG Actually Works — And Why Your AI Chatbot Keeps Hallucinating

Retrieval-Augmented Generation is the most talked-about architecture in enterprise AI right now. Here's what it actually does, where it breaks, and what to watch out for when you build with it.

Aashi Agarwal
8 min read · AI Engineering

If you've deployed an AI chatbot in the last year, you've almost certainly encountered this: the model confidently tells a user something that is completely, demonstrably wrong. It cites a policy that doesn't exist, invents a product feature, or describes a competitor's offering as your own.

This isn't a bug in the traditional sense. It's a fundamental characteristic of how large language models work — and it's the core problem that Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) was designed to solve.

This post explains what RAG actually does under the hood, why it helps, and — just as importantly — where it fails in practice. If you're building or evaluating AI products, this is the architecture you need to understand.

Why LLMs Hallucinate in the First Place

To understand RAG, you first need to understand what's causing the hallucination problem. Large language models don't store facts the way a database does. They don't "know" things in any retrievable sense.

What they do is predict the most statistically likely next token given everything that came before it. During training, they see billions of documents and develop an extraordinarily sophisticated model of language — what words tend to follow other words, in what contexts, in what styles. But that training is fixed. The model's weights are frozen after training. It has no ability to look anything up.

So when you ask a model "What is our current refund policy?" it doesn't retrieve that document from your systems. It generates an answer that sounds like a refund policy, based on all the refund policy language it saw during training. If your actual policy isn't in its training data — and it almost certainly isn't — you get a convincing hallucination.

The core problem: LLMs are prediction engines, not retrieval engines. They generate text that looks like a correct answer, not text that is necessarily a correct answer. RAG is the architectural fix for this distinction.

What RAG Actually Does

RAG solves this by giving the model something it doesn't have by default: access to current, authoritative information at the time of inference. Instead of asking the model to generate an answer from its internal weights alone, RAG adds a retrieval step before the generation step.

Here's what the pipeline looks like:

Step 1
Index
Your documents chunked and embedded into a vector store
→
Step 2
Retrieve
User query matched against vectors; top-k chunks returned
→
Step 3
Augment
Retrieved chunks injected into the prompt as context
→
Step 4
Generate
Model answers using provided context, not just its weights

The RAG pipeline: retrieval happens before generation, grounding the model's output in real documents.

Step 1: Indexing your knowledge base

Before any user query arrives, you need to prepare your documents. This means splitting them into chunks (typically 200–500 tokens each), passing each chunk through an embedding model, and storing the resulting vectors in a vector database like Pinecone, Weaviate, or pgvector.

The embedding model converts text into a dense numerical representation — a vector — where semantically similar text sits close together in vector space. This is what makes the retrieval step possible.

Step 2: Retrieving relevant context

When a user asks a question, that question is also embedded into the same vector space. The system then performs a similarity search — finding the chunks whose vectors are closest to the query vector. These are the most semantically relevant passages in your knowledge base.

Most production systems retrieve the top 3–10 chunks, though the right number depends heavily on your chunk size, context window, and the nature of your queries.

Step 3: Augmenting the prompt

The retrieved chunks are then inserted into the prompt alongside the user's question. The model is instructed (usually via a system prompt) to answer the question using only the provided context — not its general training knowledge.

Step 4: Generation grounded in context

Now the model generates. But instead of pulling from its weights alone, it's working with real, current, authoritative information you provided. If the answer isn't in the retrieved chunks, a well-prompted RAG system will say so rather than hallucinating.

Where RAG Breaks in Practice

RAG is powerful, but most implementations underperform for the same set of reasons. Here's what actually goes wrong.

Chunk size is wrong

Too small, and chunks lack the context needed to be useful. Too large, and retrieval becomes imprecise because the chunk covers too many topics. Most teams default to a fixed chunk size without evaluating whether it fits their document structure. Tables, code blocks, and lists require different chunking strategies than prose.

The retriever and the generator are mismatched

Vector similarity retrieval finds chunks that are semantically related to the query — but "semantically related" doesn't always mean "contains the answer." A question about pricing might retrieve a chunk that discusses pricing philosophy without containing actual prices. Adding a reranking step (using a cross-encoder model) significantly improves precision by scoring retrieved chunks against the actual query.

The knowledge base is stale

RAG is only as good as the documents you index. Teams often do a one-time ingestion and forget that their knowledge base needs to be kept current. A RAG system answering questions from a product knowledge base that's six months out of date will produce answers that are confidently wrong in a different way — not hallucinated, but outdated.

No citations, no trust

One of RAG's biggest advantages is that it can tell you exactly which document an answer came from. Most implementations don't surface this. Showing users the source document — even just a link or filename — dramatically increases trust and makes errors easier to catch.

Quick diagnostic: If your RAG system frequently says "I don't have information on that" when the information clearly exists in your knowledge base, your retrieval is failing. If it gives plausible-sounding wrong answers, your generation isn't being constrained enough by the retrieved context.

When RAG Isn't the Right Answer

RAG is the right architecture when your AI needs access to proprietary, current, or domain-specific knowledge. But it's not a universal solution.

  • If the model needs to reason across many documents simultaneously, RAG's top-k retrieval may miss critical context. Long-context models or graph-based approaches can do better.
  • If your knowledge base is small and stable, fine-tuning may be more effective and simpler to maintain.
  • If your queries require precise numerical lookups (exact prices, dates, figures), a traditional database with structured queries will outperform vector similarity every time.

The Bottom Line

RAG is not a feature you add to a model — it's a system you design and maintain. The retrieval quality determines the generation quality. Getting RAG right means paying as much attention to your indexing pipeline, chunk strategy, and knowledge base maintenance as you do to your model selection.

When it works, RAG gives you something that feels like magic: an AI that knows your business, answers from your documents, and can cite exactly where it got its information. When it doesn't, you get a system that's confidently wrong in ways that are harder to debug than plain hallucination.

Build the retrieval layer carefully. The generation will follow.
